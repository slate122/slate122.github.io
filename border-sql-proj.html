PART I – UNDERSTANDING DATA

1. Data Time Frame

The data represented in this report includes all legal entry methods of ground transportation from 1/1/1996 up until as recent as 3/1/2025 and is updated monthly by port.

2. Total Number of Border Entries (All Time)

During the near 30 years of data collection, a total of 11,589,879,797 ground transportations have entered the northern or southern border of the United States. This number includes passengers within vehicles and is the total count of all entrants, therefore commuters for example will likely be counted multiple times within the same month.

4. Port Distribution by Border

We can also find out how many ports of entry each border contains. The U.S. – Canada border contains 89 ports of entry and the U.S. - Mexico border Contains 28 ports of entry. This is not surprising to see that Canada contains almost 3 times the number of ports as the border is close to 3 times as long as that of Mexico’s. What is surprising is that the total number of entrants in the south is roughly 3 times that of the north. Where the Northern border is close to 25% of total entrants and the southern border is closer to 75% of total entries.

PART III – ANALYZING METHODS OF ENTRY

10. Entry Method Analysis

a. Total Entries by Method by Border

This graph shows which methods are primarily used on each border. We see very clearly that most of the entries are done using personal vehicles with pedestrians being a notable second on the southern border. It is interesting to note the difference in pedestrian crossings between the two borders. This is most likely due to more popular cities along the southern border with more walkable infrastructure and climate.

b. Method Popularity by Border

This graphic is similar in nature to the previous one but allows us to see the methods of entry broken into percentages. We can see that buses seem to be more popular in the north than in the south. We also see that there seems to be a larger percentage of both truck transport and rail transport from Canada then from Mexico. This could imply that we import more percentage wise than we do from Mexico. It also could be a statement about infrastructure that allows for the usage of rail and larger cargo trucks in the north that are not possible to the south.
